    A FRACTION OF JUSTICE

    Chapter #1: Mahogany and Jade

    Word Count: 3,053
    Read Time: Approx. 24 mins

    [Alexander’s POV]

    Tick, tock… Tick, tock… Tick… I hardly registered the familiar thrum of the ancient grandfather clock. It was the ever steady, ever present beat that forever kept tempo in this lofty room. I breathed in as deeply as my lungs would allow, the dust motes swirling in the golden shafts of afternoon sun that peered through the half closed blinds. The comforting smell of paper glue, leather binding and pages withered with age, relaxed my shoulders and renewed my focus to the task at hand. 

    As the light began to wane on the dark, wooden desktop where I stood, I sidestepped some documents splayed out, remembering the last time I’d been chastised for leaving miniature, dusty footprints on a client’s file. I deftly climbed the body of the brass and emerald table lamp, swinging from the articulated arm, and gripping the chain as I let gravity do the work of pulling my meager body weight down and turning the bulbs on with a metallic clunk. I swung there for a moment, enjoying the slightly higher view. I wouldn’t normally horse around like this, but even I deserved a momentary break every now and again, especially when no one was around to see.

    I craned my neck peering at the glass cabinet that loomed like a skyscraper above me and to my right, containing countless accolades, degrees, and a variety of wonders from around the world, collected from the old man’s travels in his early days. I envied him. He was human. The world was his oyster. If he wanted to visit a foreign country, he could step on a plane and find himself there. 

    He’d never allowed me inside the cabinet, even after all these years, so I’d memorized the things I could see from afar: A Fabergé egg, from Moscow, a stone box carved with rare jewels from New Delhi, a fragment of marble from the Roman Forum and, my personal favorite, which glared at me with its ruby red eye, glinting in the dying light, a jade sculpture of a dragon, from Beijing. Its serpentine body writhed and curled around itself. I couldn’t begin to wrap my brain around these fantastical objects and the far away cultures they represented, not really. No matter how much I read from the extensive library which cascaded from floor to ceiling along two of the four walls of this vast and impressive office, it would never come close to the experience of breathing in the foreign air and setting foot on the soil of a different land. My heart thundered in my chest. Oh, to be human. I reached out my arm, its length only measuring a meager few inches. I’d never been eye level with the green beast, but I imagined it was as big as I was tall, if not bigger. I tried to picture what it must be like to palm it in my hand, running my finger tips over its cascading scales and thrashing body. How much easier life must be when the world is customized perfectly to you. 

    With a clamor, the old man practically crashed through the door. He was getting slower and clumsier by the day. I almost felt bad for him. His cane clunked loudly on the creaking wooden floor, while the characteristically squeaky wheel of his oxygen tank squealed rhythmically as it was dragged behind his shuffling gate. Despite his crooked, hunched spine, his hands gnarled and bony with advanced arthritis, his practically bald head which only sported a ghostly tuft of hair where a luxurious mane had once reigned supreme, in spite of these significant signs of a man entering the final chapter of his life, he maintained a dignity and an air of superiority. He insisted on wearing a three piece woolen suit, his collar and tie somehow in perfect alignment even with the state of his stiffened hands. The suit was a mottled brown, with a crimson tie and white dress shirt beneath. His shoes an inky, reflective black. 

    As he made his journey from the door to the desk chair, I let go of the chain where I’d been dangling, which left me about ten inches off the surface of the desk, a bit less than double my own body length. The drop was not insignificant, but, neither was it painful. I was desperate to busy myself and maintain the illusion that I’d been steadily working, my palms growing clammy as my mind panicked. I knew I’d suffer the consequences if he thought I’d been slacking off. I fell to my hands and knees, poring over the contents of a document, not one word of which I was actually able to retain. My heart pounded against my ribs. 

    “I saw that-“ he snapped in his gravelly, dry voice. My blood froze in my veins, but I stayed crouched down, too scared to meet his gaze. “I keep you to perform a job, not to swing from my furniture like a frivolous child at play. I may be old, but I’m not blind.” Well, that was debatable. But he clearly could see well enough to catch me in the act. Grunting painfully, the old man half lowered, half dropped himself into his massive, tufted leather and gold augmented chair. The sudden collapse of his form sent a rush of air across the desk which fluttered the papers and rustled my hair. I clenched my jaw, I had to face the music. 

    I stood to attention, staring at my own leather dress shoes, a caramel brown that stood out from the dark stain of the wood beneath them. I didn’t dare to lift my eyes, “Yes sir, I’m sorry, sir. I was only turning on the lamp so we could continue—“ 

    Suddenly, swifter than the breadth of a heartbeat, the old man had gripped a pen between his grizzled fingers, the body of which he snapped toward me, aimed at my throat, I scrambled, falling back, my hands flying in front of my face. I found myself suddenly trapped beneath the writing utensil  and the unforgiving, hard surface of the desk, the body of the pen pressing into my neck. I didn’t dare to squirm in protest. “Do I find you in contempt of this court, Alexander?” I struggled to steady my breathing, as the pressure on my larynx increased. His face was absolutely expressionless as if this act of discipline meant no more to him than the most mundane of tasks, just something that had to be done to get what he wanted. 

    Choking now, I spat out, “N-no, s-s-sir.” He pressed a bit harder, I coughed and sputtered. Then, he let go. My hands flew to my throat as I gasped for air. I prided myself on being a model aide, so to have been caught making a mistake was a significantly greater blow to my pride than my physical body. My face flushed hot with embarrassment. Before I had a chance to recover my composure, that same writing utensil was prodding me in the spine. I leapt to my feet. 

    “Get yourself in order. You look no better than a junior associate on his first case. Show some respect.” I took pride in my impeccable appearance. Having so much as a thread out of place was enough to make my eye twitch. Where did I adopt that personality trait from? I wondered, begrudgingly, as I tucked my navy tie back where it belonged and adjusted my electric blue suit jacket to rest symmetrically over my shoulders. 

    In the twenty years I’d known the old man, he’d never once uttered a word of encouragement or kindness, not to his nursing staff, not to his son who had only visited him once in my memory and certainly not to a pathetic little pet like me. I honestly didn’t blame the son, although I remember him being a piece of work in his own right, just as most men who didn’t earn but inherited exorbitant wealth so often were. 

    The ancient curmudgeon was anything but soft. Yet, for all that, he’d single-handedly saved my life. When the groundskeeper had been ready to feed me to his snapping, drooling mastiff, the old man had shook his head, smirking at me, bemused at the child he saw dangling upside down from the groundskeeper’s gloved fingers, staring back at him with wide and frightened eyes. 

    I had him to thank for everything I had… my bed, tucked cozily between a 1925 copy of Ulysses and a leather-bound volume of The Odyssey, for example.

    “A fitting reminder of what a sense of adventure can do to a man” he’d always mused, “…remember, Circe can turn you into a little snorting piglet just as easily as I might be transformed into a bristled boar…” I had supposed that was some strange metaphor to imply the world was dangerous outside the library. I didn’t need much convincing of that, given my previous encounters out in the open. In addition to a place to rest my head, I had my trim and fashionable suits, never lacking in fine detail. I was fed, given access to clean, fresh water.  I was shielded from the immediate dangers of the outside world. But most importantly, I had him to thank for knowledge. When he’d taken me in at age ten, and I’d shown a thirst for learning, he’d seen that as a golden opportunity to make use of me.

    Even back then, the old man had begun to develop poorer and poorer vision. Especially when it came to reading fine print. That’s where I came in, ever more necessary as the years wore on and the cataracts continued to destroy his ocular lenses. I was proud of my work. Well, to clarify, I was proud of what my work gave me access to. He may have never said it, but I knew he relied on me, that I was helping as he built cases and gave legal counsel to the new partners of his firm. Now, twenty years on, he was practically blind, certainly when it came to anything up close. That’s why I’d been so shocked when he’d pinned me down with such well-aimed ease. 

    Determined to show my worth, I rolled my shoulders back, and got to work, bending over documents that spread out all around me, as I articulated each and every syllable, loud and clear, with crisp, perfect diction, just as he’d taught me all that time ago. If I took too long to flip a page over or move to the other side of the document to read a footnote, I’d receive a blunt thump on the back of my head. Wanting to make up for my earlier embarrassment, I made sure he never had reason to smack me for wasting his time. 

    We worked for hours, I’d repeat and pause where he asked, then, I’d take the notes he dictated to me on a smart phone propped upright on a stand. The keyboard was still overwhelmingly huge for my small hands. In fact, standing toe to toe with the device, the keyboard display still came up just shy of my hips. Despite that, however, I’d learned to type with lightning speed and accuracy, determined to do my job as well as I could manage. 

    This was all I had known, for twenty years of my life. For two-thirds of my life, I’d played the role of a desk tool for a giant with an anger management problem. Trips to China and Russia were not in the cards for someone who measured five and a half inches from head to toe. So, I did the best that I could to survive here, in this insulated bubble of tailored suits and legalese. But I wasn’t satisfied with simply fulfilling my expected obligations. No, by empowering me with knowledge of the written word, this elderly man had ignited a fire that he’d never be able to stamp out, no matter how hard he tried. I had a trick up my sleeve, two decades in the making. And when the time was right, I’d show him my cards and change my fate. But for now, I had to work to earn my keep. 

    More time ticked by, my heart clunking in rhythm to that clock in the corner: tick, tock, tick, tock, tick…. I was exhausted, running around the desk reading as quickly yet clearly as I could, having to be extra loud now due to his hearing loss, typing notes as fast as my little hands could manage. But, of course, I never let any weakness show. I was his perfect, albeit tiny, aide. We worked until he said stop. Despite his advanced age, the man rarely tired. It amazed me how long he managed to stay sharp and awake. We worked well into the night, as sweat formed on my brow and my bones ached. I was violently hungry, and my throat was as dry as if I’d swallowed a fistful of sand. But I dared not ask for a single thing, lest I wanted to be choked or beaten again.

    Finally, he leaned back in his chair, his fingers playing wistfully over his chin, as if contemplating his next chess move. This was always how I knew he was finished for the night. He pocketed the cellphone and, gripping the desk for help, slowly, painfully, rose to standing, before shuffling off to his adjacent bedroom. He didn’t say a word to me as he mumbled to himself. When he reached the edge of the desk, he flicked the light off (with ease, of course. Which I noted with jealousy) and I was left, on the desk top, plunged into pitch black darkness, save for slivers of silvery moonlight peering in through one, distant window. I sighed, he may have been ready to call it a night, but my work was far from done. 

    I sat down right where I was, turning toward the illuminated strip of light emanating from the ornate French doors of his room. I fixed my eyes on that crack of gold illuminating the floor; as soon as it disappeared, I could get to work on the secret I’d somehow managed to keep from him for years. 

    I rubbed the back of my neck, my eyelids as heavy as lead, threatening to seal my dry and reddened eyes shut for the night, even while sitting upright. C’mon you doddering old coot… any time now… I drummed my fingers on the desk, twitching with impatience. Then…. Darkness. Perfect.

    Crawling on hands and knees, I felt around blindly until I bumped into the base of the table lamp. Feeling for the familiar handholds, I clamored up the arm and found the chain again. That stupid chain that’d no doubt earned me a purple bruise around my neck. I leapt from the safety of the metal arm, gripping the chain. I fell for a moment, gliding through the air before… thunk the give in the chain went taught and I was suspended. As soon as the pendulum swing had settled, I jumped down and landed, softly, on the balls of my feet. Now the desktop was flooded with golden, electrical light. Whew. It was a lot more work to simply turn on a light at my size! I wiped my brow with the back of my hand, surveying the desk practically buried in documents and files.

    I knelt down, sliding my fingers up the bottom of my pants leg on the right side, feeling for that familiar strip of ribbon. Untying it, a leaflet of loose papers fell into my waiting palm. I cleared some space on the desktop and, sitting down I thumbed through my body of work. Spread out in my lap were scraps of paper I’d managed to pillage from the trash can over time, ripped into pieces small enough to wrap around my leg, hidden away from human eyes. Every spec of usable space was taken advantage of, as I tried my best to write as small as I possibly could. Years worth of meticulous notes, citations and drafts of arguments for one conclusive purpose: a case for the emancipation and civil rights of petkind. At least, that was the working title for now. 

    Of course, since I had to do my work in secret, and I had the disadvantage of only being able to use what was already opened and available to me, the work was slow. Painfully slow. There was so much I had to teach myself to even understand the legal jargon I was reading in book after book and file after file. Still, I was determined to one day argue my case before the Supreme Court itself. My chest swelled just thinking about it. 

    I found a page with a fresh, blank side and fished for the lump of broken pencil lead in my pocket. I always kept these things about my person: the journal and the makeshift pencil, that is, unless, I was being bathed that day, in which case I stashed them under my bed. I didn’t trust the old man not to go snooping around, and certainly not his twittering nurses who flitted in and out of this house each day like birds on a migratory path for the winter. I knew if anyone here discovered what I was doing before I’d had the chance to defend it, they’d take it away and, worst case scenario, I might even get kicked out of the house. 

    Still, it was something I felt I had to do. It seemed to me that most pets lived their lonely and pathetic lives trying desperately just to survive. The old man had been working in property law for almost seventy years, and each case that came across his desk involving pets was burned into my mind. Cases of blatant torture and abuse, documented on video, broadcast live on social media. Sex trafficking, murder. You name it, humans had done it. Hell, they did it to each other, why would they have qualms doing those things to a race they could subdue with a flick of their wrists? 

    I recognized the solemn duty I had to each and every one of those victims and to the thousands more who were suffering in silence. What other pet had this knowledge at his fingertips, and access to the information necessary to arm himself with a rock solid legal argument? My education was my weapon to fight the injustice being done to us on a systemic scale, and I intended to wield it soon. Quite soon. 

    I shuddered remembering how in every instance the old man had argued in favor of the human abuser, continually striking down proposed bills on the rights of pets as sentient creatures, arguing in favor of the upheld precedent that pets were property, not people. The first case like this I had been forced to read aloud came through his desk when I was a teenager, I’m not sure how old, maybe sixteen. I remember my blood boiling in my veins as I realized the man who’d taken me in didn’t give a damn about me. Not really. I was a tool, nothing more. That had set this project in motion. And ever since, every night, I’d read what I could get my hands on. 

    I knew the old man’s legal track record. I could see, perhaps better than anyone else, how little he thought of people like me. Still, he was nothing if not precise in his work. I hoped, once I could finish the final summary of my argument, to convince him I was right. Not by pulling at his heart strings and igniting his compassion, because as far as I could see he possessed neither, but, rather, to impress him with my meticulous research into the constitutionality of the current legal precedent. If I could convince him, there was a chance my case could see the light of day. 

    I scoured the desk for the article I’d gotten halfway though the night before. I traveled across the wide surface, bemused as my purple and deep blue shadow was flung across the lines of black ink on crisp white. I wasn’t even half as tall as this sheet of paper on which I stood. I searched and searched, before coming to the conclusion that the old man must’ve filed it away. Dammit. I needed that. Oh well, nothing I could do.

    I sucked fresh air into my lungs, falling to my hands and knees and preparing to dive into a new case entirely to see what else I could learn. Just as I was finishing the abstract, a sound made me jump. I froze in place, trying to anticipate just what I’d heard.
