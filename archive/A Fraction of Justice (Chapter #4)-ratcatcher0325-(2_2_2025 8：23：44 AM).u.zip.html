Who’s at the door?Previous: Chapter #3Next: Chapter #5CW: Adult language, dehumanization, mild abuse, all the angst________________________________________A FRACTION OF JUSTICEChapter #4: InheritanceWord Count: 1,593Read Time: Approx. 12 mins[Alexander’s POV]I stood frozen like a prey animal in the face of a natural predator, for just a fraction of a second. I recognized how hereditary instinct was taking over my intellect. My whole body told me to cower, run, hide! And I let my nervous system do the thinking for me. Shimmying back into the dark shadows, I pressed all the way into the back wall of the shelf, shrouding myself in the darkness and curling up to make myself as small as possible. At the last second, I stood up and grabbed my makeshift bag, hugging it close to my chest, before cowering down in the shadows once more. A figure entered the room, one I didn’t immediately recognize. He was sloppily dressed, in tennis shoes, ill-fitting jeans, a nondescript hoodie, and a too-large suit jacket over that. His jet black hair was receding into a significant widow’s peak, and his goatee was showing some flecks of grey. The old man’s son. He’d only ever visited once and that was ten years ago. He’d aged a great deal since then. He was the polar opposite to his father in many ways, clearly his appearance was sloppy, and he seemed to have no drive to accomplish anything, but he had successfully inherited his father’s impatience and lack of compassion. I remembered back to the last time I’d seen him, when he’d thrown a literal temper tantrum in the office, over his allowance that had been reduced by a few thousand dollars that month. He’d been in his late forties then. I’d watched with wide-eyed terror as he’d flung books, papers and pens across the room with wild abandon. I’d been almost certain I was next, he’d even eyed me as I stared up at him, but the old man had stood up, slammed his fist on the table and the infantilized middle-aged man had stopped in his tracks, cowering and blubbering like an idiot. Now, this pathetic man-child I saw before me was anything but afraid. In fact, as I watched him scratch his own crotch with a total lack of shame (of course, he thought he was alone) and survey the room, I could see his chest was puffed out as a smile curled his lips. All of this was his now. All this wealth he hadn’t earned, he could now squander as he pleased. His father was by no means a compassionate or loving parent, but I did find it fascinating that in twenty years he had only bothered to visit once, yet not twelve hours after his death, he’d come to claim his prize. I swallowed as he directed his steps towards the shelf, his brow furrowed as he looked at something on the ground. I had to stay hidden and find a way to slip out undetected. It shouldn’t be hard. Most humans were quite stupid and he was chief among them. After all, I had no intention of being a part of this man’s inherited belongings. He sauntered forward, head still trained on the ground. The book! He’d seen the book I’d pushed off the shelf. My heart thundered in my chest. I hoped he’d just set it down on the desk and pass me by.  He was lazy, after all, wouldn’t he do what took the least amount of effort? Maybe he wouldn’t even bother picking it up at all. I watched every move with wide eyes. He stopped, each foot planted on either side of the haphazardly thrown object. He scratched his chin before bending over. As he rose back up, his eyes slowly followed the trajectory of the fallen item. My heart leapt in my throat. He stared dumbly, taking in one shelf after another as though earnestly having to try solving the simplest puzzle known to man. And I say man, specifically, because I was more and more convinced every second that passed in this one’s presence that pets couldn’t possibly be this stupid. Finally, he stared at the place I was hiding. I froze. His eyes were trained directly on me. I fought every urge to run and tried to stay as still as possible, holding my breath. Despite looking at exactly where I was hiding, his eyes did not shimmer with recognition. If I just stayed silent maybe he’d put the book back and move on. My lungs were burning, my eyes filling with tears. My muscles screamed as I held myself stock still. He stared back at the book in his hand as if finally recognizing this as its place on the shelf. Please, please just let him put it back halfheartedly and be done. I knew that if he tried to slide it all the way back, I’d be in the way. But I had nowhere to go. All I could do was hope against hope that his laziness would favor me. The light was blotted out as pages slid into the space before me. The book came closer. I was trembling from head to toe. Closer. I scrambled as far back as I could. Closer. It was millimeters from me now. It wasn’t going to stop. I bit the inside of my cheek to keep from making a sound of distress. The book pressed up against my body until there was nowhere else to go. I was almost never wrong, but in this instance I was willing to admit I’d made a huge mistake. I’d had my chance to escape and now here I was about to experience a brand new kind of misery I couldn’t even imagine. “… the hell?” I heard him mutter. The pressure released for a fraction of a second before… wham! He slammed the book into me, crushing me between the force of his hand and the wall. All the air was forced from my lungs. He drew back and crushed me again, with even more force this time. It was excruciating. The third time, I couldn’t help but cry out in pain. “The fuck??” Light flooded in again until it was replaced by a giant blue eye straining to look me over in the dark. His brow was furrowed, and then, suddenly, his iris glinted with recognition. “Oh yeah… I forgot about you… c’mere…” rough, sweaty fingers that could hardly fit jammed their way to the back of the shelf, and try as I might to avoid his grasp, his finger and thumb managed to lock on to my ankle, before pulling me violently forward. I was still gripping defensively to my bag, hoping it could at least serve as a buffer between my body and his rough hands. As he dragged me to the lip of the shelf, I squirmed and writhed, but it was of no use. He pinched the collar of my jacket and I was airborne, dangling what seemed to be miles above the wooden floor, trapped and absolutely terrified. “What do you have there?” “No, n-no, please—“ He ripped the bag from my hands as easily as tearing a petal from a rose. I couldn’t fight him on it even if I’d wanted to. He rolled it in his fingers with a puzzled look before shrugging and tossing it in the trash can behind him. My breath caught in my throat. “Look at you…” he was examining me now, rotating his wrist to look at me from all sides.  I hung there, dangling limply, my face flushed hot as I boiled with rage. He roughy shoved a finger against my ribs to pull my lapel away from my side so he could see the other layers of my outfit. I twisted and writhed at his touch. “D-don’t! Just put me down!” He chuckled at this, finding my protestations adorable, which made me even angrier. He simply scratched his chin, “I really had forgotten all about you. The little pencil pusher. I never got why dad kept you…” he pulled me closer, his breath reeked of garlic and bacteria. I scowled. “I mean, fuck, he dressed you up just like him. Fucking full of himself all the way to the end, I guess. Figures.” He shook his head. He seemed to notice the trash can at his heel, and a smile painted his features. I held my breath. “Now what do we do with you huh?” In a surprisingly swift move, I found myself lurching forward, choking on my own collar as he dropped to a crouch, dangling me out above the plastic lined opening of the bin. “No need for a little Poindexter anymore… guess we can retire ya…” he shook his hand and my body flailed in response. As I gasped between being flung about, I cried out in protest.“P-please no! N-no, I can- I can help, I—“ he dropped his grip suddenly, making it seem like he’d let go. I wailed with fright, my hands pulling at my tie that was strangling me. He roared with laughter. His lips parted as he seemed about to say something else, when another sound ricocheted through the air.  This time, high pitched, and whiny, two more human voices echoed in my ears, “Dad, where are you???” “Dad, we’re bored, this house is old and weird!”Oh no. Children. There was absolutely no creature more dangerous to pets on this earth than human children. The man holding me captive glanced up at the sounds of his sons’ voices, and I saw his gears turning (slowly, but turning nonetheless). “Boys! Come in here!
